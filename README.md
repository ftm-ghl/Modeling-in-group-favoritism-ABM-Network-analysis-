# Modeling-in-group-favoritism (ABM and Network analysis)

## My master's thesis project: 

### Modeling the Effect of In-group Favoritism on Social Decision-Making

###### Humans are inherently social beings and groups play an important role in decision making. Individuals derive part of their social identity and self-esteem from a sense of belonging to groups (De Dreu, 2021; Konovalov & Craf, 2021). One of the most critical social decisions is whether to cooperate with others. Social norms such as the norm of reciprocity are vital in deciding whether to cooperate or avoid exploitation in social interactions. However, these norms can lead to biases like in-group favoritism (Kurzban et al., 2015; Tilstead et al., 2019; Rahal et al., 2020).

##### In-group favoritism is a cognitive bias that leads to greater trust and a higher preference to cooperate with members of their own group compared to others (Yakovlev & Spears, 2018; Dorheim et al., 2022; Mullerstorm et al., 2022). According to Tajfel's minimal group paradigm and social categorization theory, merely classifying individuals into distinct groups can trigger in-group favoritism, significantly influencing our decisions, particularly in social contexts (Tajfel & Turner, 1971, 2004; Durrheim et al., 2016; Abbink & Harris, 2019).



### Research Objectives
To model the evolution of in-group favoritism and reciprocity over time.

### Research Hypotheses
1.	The intensity of in-group favoritism increases over time in social interactions.
2.	Reciprocity increases over time in social interactions.
3.	In-group favoritism emerges faster than reciprocal intergroup cooperation in social interactions.
